1990 — 2019 Internal Flows in Germany
1991 — 2019
Most people move over relatively short distances, such as within Europe, Asia and Africa. Emigrants from North America and Europe moved to many different destinations across many world regions, while people in the Former Soviet Union moved predominantly within the same region.
Europe and the Former Soviet Union are the busiest world regions in terms of migration. Compared to the previous period (1990-1995), immigration to North America increased the most, while movements within South Asia decreased the most.
Europe emerges as an important migration hub, with increasing immigration from North America, Latin America, and South Asia. The Agreement on the Free Movement of People within Europe also contributes to rising migration flows within the continent.
Asia joins Europe and North America as a third migration hub, with emigration from South Asia increasing strongly due to soaring demand for labour migrants from India and other South Asian countries.
Caused by the armed conflict in Syria, migration within and from West Asia rises strongly.
Migration from West Asia to Europe and also within Europe continues to increase notably. Armed conflicts and civil unrest drive up migration within Latin America.
Africa, South Asia and Europe are by far the largest host regions for refugees. The largest flows of refugees occur within Africa and South Asia. The Fall of the Soviet Union led to sizable outflows of refugees to Europe, mostly Germany. The origin country of these refugees is classified as “unknown” in official statistics until 2002.
Africa, Europe and South Asia are the centres of refugee movements. Compared to the previous period, movements doubled within Europe, where there are ongoing conflicts in countries of former Yugoslavia. Sizable movements also occurred within the former Soviet Union. In contrast, refugee numbers in Africa and South Asia decreased moderately.
Refugee movements within West Asia increase substantially due to the war in Afghanistan. The global trend continues to be dominated by refugee flows within Africa and to Europe.
The refugee movements to Europe are lower than in the previous period. The refugee flows within West Asia surge due to the war in Iraq.
Due to the armed conflict in Syria, refugee flows in West Asia increase even further compared to the previous period. While refugees in Africa and South Asia mainly move within the region, Europe records more refugees from non-European countries.
Refugee flows within West Asia more than doubled compared to the previous period. Syria is an important origin of refugees in Turkey, in other countries in West Asia, as well as in Europe. Afghanistan is a major origin of refugees in South Asia. South Sudan and Myanmar emerge as new key origins of refugees.
The years after German reunification are characterized by sizable migration from East to West. Besides, large cities experience net migration losses to the hinterland.
The suburbanisation trend observed after German reunification continues. Especially cities in East Germany lose population to adjacent regions. This becomes most evident for Berlin, which loses more than twice as many people to adjacent Brandenburg as in the early 1990s.
The largest cities start to gain population through internal migration as the suburbanisation trend from the mid-1990s comes to an end. Berlin is an exception to this trend: the capital city still experiences high outflows to Brandenburg that result in a net loss of population.
The spatial pattern of flows continues to be characterised by urbanisation. Cities gain population through internal migration, while hinterlands and rural regions record population losses.
The urbanisation trend of the 2000s comes to an end, although a few cities such as Berlin continue to experience population gains through internal migration. Many cities experience balanced in- and outflows, while other cities like Munich lose population.
Large cities experience increasing net migration losses to the hinterland, a pattern that is commonly described as suburbanisation. Leipzig and other cities in the East are noteworthy exceptions to this trend, as they continue to gain population through internal migration.
